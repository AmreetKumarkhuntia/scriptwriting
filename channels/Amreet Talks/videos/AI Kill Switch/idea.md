## Body Outline — 5 Chained Points (Ascending Shock), each ends in a re-hook

**Point 1 — WHAT happened: 72 hours from launch to lights-out**
The timeline. June 9: Anthropic ships Claude Fable 5, its most powerful public model ever. June 12, ~5:21 PM Friday: a letter from Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick to CEO Dario Amodei. Within hours, Fable 5 *and* Mythos 5 are dark — not just for some users, for everyone on the planet. First time in history a government has reached in and switched off a frontier AI that was already live.
*Re-hook:* "Now — your first guess is probably 'safety bug.' And kind of. But *who* found that bug is where this gets uncomfortable."

**Point 2 — THE TRIGGER: the call came from inside the house**
A "highly credible trusted partner" demonstrated a jailbreak — got the model to cough up restricted cyberattack info. Reporting says that partner was Amazon — one of Anthropic's own biggest investors. Andy Jassy reportedly took it straight to the White House. The ask (per David Sacks): fix it or pull it. Anthropic refused. So the government pulled it for them.
*Re-hook:* "And to do that, they reached for a law from 2018 that was never, ever meant for this. That's where the switch comes from."

**Point 3 — THE MECHANISM: an export law became a global off-switch**
The Export Control Reform Act — the rules built to stop the US shipping missile tech and chips abroad. Commerce's BIS arm applied it to a *model.* The order: no access for any "foreign national," anywhere. But Anthropic can't sort its users by passport — so the only way to comply was to shut it down for the entire world. A US export rule, written for hardware, just remotely switched off software running on everyone's screen.
*Re-hook:* "Which leaves the question that actually matters: once a switch like that exists — who's holding it?"

**Point 4 — WHO HOLDS THE SWITCH: it's not Anthropic anymore**
Here's the quiet bombshell. Fable 5 doesn't come back when Anthropic decides — it comes back when the government grants a *license.* They flew Tom Brown and their policy chief to Washington to negotiate the return of their own product. Meanwhile Palantir — running Claude on a billion dollars of defense contracts — has to rip it out and rebuild. The lab that built its whole brand on *please regulate us, AI is too dangerous* just watched that exact argument become the lever that took its model away. Their safety pitch didn't protect them. It armed the people who switched them off.
*Re-hook:* "But here's the twist that flips this entire story — because there's one kind of AI this switch can never reach."

**Point 5 — THE KICKER: you can't switch off what's already downloaded**
A kill switch only works on a closed model — one company, one set of servers, one plug to pull. It does nothing to open weights already sitting on a million hard drives. Mistral's response was basically: fine, we'll go open. China's models *are* open. So the lesson the world just learned is brutal for the US: closed American AI can be switched off by Washington — open Chinese AI can't be switched off by anyone. The ban meant to protect American AI dominance might be the best ad open-source has ever had. *(Land the thesis — callback to the China video.)*
