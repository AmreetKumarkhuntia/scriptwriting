# Script: The AI Bubble

**Status:** Draft v1

**Language:** English

## HOOK (0:00–0:25)

I want to show you some math.

Not complicated math. Just the kind nobody in tech seems to want to talk about.

Because when you look at what it actually costs to run ChatGPT — and then look at what you pay for it — there's a gap.

A massive one.

And my theory? Someone is filling that gap right now. And at some point — they stop.

Here's what I think happens next.

---

## THE SUBSIDY (0:25–1:15)

Let's start with something I can actually verify.

Every AI company publishes their API pricing. Public information. Anyone can check it.

Here's what it costs to run Claude Opus — Anthropic's most powerful model — for one power user in a month.

At 2 million tokens, you're looking at roughly **$60 in raw compute**.

The subscription price? **$20.**

That $40 gap doesn't disappear. Someone pays it.

Right now, that someone is Microsoft — which has put over $13 billion into OpenAI. And Amazon, which has put $4 billion into Anthropic. These companies are subsidizing your AI conversations. Deliberately. Because they're buying your habit before the real price kicks in.

This isn't a conspiracy. It's a strategy. Get you dependent. Then figure out the economics later.

The question is: what happens when "later" arrives?

---

## THE CHINA PROBLEM (1:15–2:00)

Because something just changed that makes "later" feel a lot closer.

China entered the chat.

DeepSeek — a Chinese AI lab — is currently offering API access at **$0.14 per million tokens**.

Claude Opus output pricing? **$25 per million.**

That's an **89x price gap.**

And DeepSeek isn't a toy. It's competitive with Western frontier models on coding benchmarks.

Google already flinched. In June 2026, they cut their AI subscription price by 40%.

OpenAI now faces an impossible choice. Match Chinese prices — and burn through money even faster. Or hold their prices — and watch users leave.

There's no good version of this for them.

---

## THE BURN (2:00–2:45)

Now — this next part is speculation. I want to be upfront about that.

Multiple reports, citing leaked internal documents, suggest OpenAI's losses this year run into the tens of billions. Their own projections apparently show they don't turn profitable until 2030. And to get there, they need to raise something like $100 billion more.

I can't verify those exact numbers. But here's the thing — even if they're half right, the pattern is alarming.

Because unlike Amazon, which lost money building warehouses it owned — OpenAI is losing money on a product that gets *more expensive* every time someone uses it.

That's a different kind of problem.

---

## THE PARALLEL (2:45–3:15)

People keep saying this is dot-com 2.0. I don't think that's quite right.

The dot-com NASDAQ was trading at a P/E ratio of 200. Today it's around 30. The companies building AI infrastructure — Microsoft, Google, Amazon — are profitable. They're not burning venture capital on hope.

My take: the bubble isn't AI. The technology is real.

The bubble is specific companies that still haven't figured out how to make money on their core product.

---

## THE VERDICT (3:15–3:50)

So who do I think survives?

NVIDIA looks safe. Real revenue. Real margins.

Cloud infrastructure — AWS, Azure, Google Cloud — safe. Enterprise contracts, profitable parents.

Anthropic is the surprise. By most reports, they hit their first profitable quarter in mid-2026.

OpenAI worries me most. Biggest brand. Biggest burn. Most exposed to Chinese competition. No hardware moat.

I could be wrong about all of this.

But the math I can verify — the pricing gap, the China pressure, the infrastructure bottlenecks — all points in the same direction.
